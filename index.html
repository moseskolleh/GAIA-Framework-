GAIA Framework 2.0
Green AI Assessment

A science-based framework for measuring, judging, and reducing the environmental footprint of AI use.

Download the Excel tool Read the specification

Every number carries a source, a vintage, and a data-quality tier. Every result is reported as low / central / high. No composite scores.

The method is the name: Ground → Assess → Interpret → Act

Four modules take an organization from "we use AI somewhere" to a sourced, uncertainty-bounded footprint with a ranked plan to reduce it.

G

Ground

Inventory your AI use: models, deployment path, hosting region, monthly volume, and token profile. Declare functional units and the system boundary.

A

Assess

Compute energy, dual-reported carbon, and water per functional unit — always as low / central / high, at the best available data-quality tier.

I

Interpret

Grade each use case A–E within its task class, run the three-question frugality check, and place totals in real-world context.

A

Act

Pick evidence-ranked mitigation levers, set intensity targets — not totals — and report with the SCI-compatible disclosure template.

    Built on real science

    GAIA does not replace measurement standards — it builds on them. Every energy figure is labeled with the tier of evidence it came from, and the tier sets the width of the uncertainty interval.

    central ×/÷ 1.15 T1 — Measured

    Direct metering of your own deployment (CodeCarbon, nvidia-smi plus host power). The gold standard; recommended for any self-hosted workload.

    central ×/÷ 1.5 T2 — Disclosed

    Provider-published full-stack measurement with a stated boundary — e.g. Google's Gemini per-prompt disclosure, Mistral's audited LCA.

    central ×/÷ 2 T3 — Benchmarked

    Independent standardized benchmarks (AI Energy Score; Jegham et al. API measurements). GPU-only figures are corrected with a serving-stack multiplier.

    central ×/÷ 3 T4 — Modeled

    Parameter-count physics (FLOPs → joules via hardware efficiency) for models with no measurement — the estimation logic of EcoLogits and Epoch AI.

    Standards GAIA implements or maps onto

    • ISO/IEC 21031:2024 — Software Carbon Intensity (SCI) · the carbon-rate-per-functional-unit specification; every GAIA result restates as an SCI score (E, I, M, R map one-to-one).
    • SCI for AI (Green Software Foundation, 2025) · the ratified AI extension of SCI; GAIA's Assess module is an implementation of it.
    • ISO 14040/14044 · life-cycle assessment principles governing GAIA's system boundary and cut-off declarations.
    • ITU-T L.1410 · ICT-sector LCA boundary and allocation rules that GAIA's scope declarations follow.
    • AFNOR SPEC 2314 — Frugal AI (2024) · source of the necessity / right-sizing / token-discipline frugality check ("question the need").
    • GHG Protocol (Scope 2 Guidance) · dual location- and market-based carbon reporting, never blended; GAIA totals feed Scope 2/3 inventory line items.

    Where GAIA sits among frameworks

    GAIA is designed to be restatable in the other instruments' terms — the crosswalk is the point. What none of them provides together: uncertainty-tiered estimates without provider cooperation, water alongside carbon, task-conditioned grading, an explicit frugality check, and an Excel artifact a non-programmer can run.

    Known limitations, stated plainly

    • Provider opacity. Closed providers do not disclose per-model serving data; most model rows are T3/T4 with wide bounds. That is a property of the industry, not the framework — the tier column makes the opacity itself visible.
    • Benchmark ≠ production. Benchmarks measure specific hardware and serving configurations; production fleets differ — batching, quantization, and speculative decoding can shift per-token energy several-fold in either direction.
    • Annual-average carbon. Grid intensity averages ignore hourly variation; marginal-emissions accounting is out of scope for v2.0.
    • Water is the weakest data. EWIF varies by basin and season, and water-stress context — where a litre is drawn — matters as much as volume. GAIA reports volume and flags stress-region hosting qualitatively.
    • Rebound effects (efficiency → more usage) are real and unmodeled; the frugality flag is the partial control.
